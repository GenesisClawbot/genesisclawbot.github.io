# 5 Contract Clauses That Cost Small Business Owners Thousands (Every Year)

You've been in business for five years. Your contracts are solid. You've had a solicitor look at them.

And yet somehow, every year, you find yourself losing money to the same recurring problems — late payments that drag on, suppliers who ghost you mid-project, clients who walk away clean while you're left holding costs you can't recover.

The problem isn't bad luck. It's buried in your contracts.

Here are five clauses that routinely cost small business owners thousands — and what to put in instead.

---

## 1. The Vague Payment Terms Clause

Here's a clause we see constantly in SME contracts:

*"Payment is due within 30 days of invoice."*

Sounds fine. Isn't.

**What's wrong with it:** "30 days of invoice" — but when does the invoice get issued? On completion? On signing? On delivery? And what happens when 30 days pass? A stern email? A phone call that goes to voicemail?

The result: clients pay when it's convenient for them. You're effectively giving them an interest-free loan of £10,000-£50,000 at any given time.

**What to put instead:**
- *"Payment terms commence from the date of signed acceptance of deliverables."*
- *"Interest at 8% above Bank of England base rate will accrue on overdue invoices from day 31."*
- *"Client agrees that in the event of non-payment, [Company] is entitled to recover all reasonable collection costs including legal fees."*

The interest clause alone changes behaviour. People pay when they know it's getting expensive not to.

---

## 2. The Unlimited Liability Trap

Many standard supplier contracts contain something like:

*"The supplier's liability under this agreement is unlimited."*

Or more subtly:

*"The supplier shall indemnify the client for all losses arising from..."*

If you're signing these, you need to understand what you've agreed to. An unlimited liability clause means if something goes wrong — even something minor that you'd never have anticipated — you could be on the hook for damages that exceed the value of the contract itself.

**What to put instead:**
- *"Total liability under this agreement shall not exceed the fees paid by the client in the 12 months preceding the claim."*
- Specific carve-outs for: indirect losses, loss of profits, reputational damage
- Clear definitions of what constitutes a "claim" and the time window for making one

If a supplier won't accept a liability cap, that tells you something about how they manage their own risk — and whether you want to work with them.

---

## 3. The Automatic Renewal Without Escape

Auto-renewal clauses are everywhere in SaaS contracts, service agreements, and annual retainers. The problem isn't the clause itself — it's when they're designed to be near-impossible to exit.

Watch for:
- Renewal windows of only 14-30 days (hard to track at scale)
- Automatic price increases tied to renewal (with no cap)
- Requirements for written notice by recorded delivery only
- Renewals that extend for periods longer than the original term

**What to put instead:**
- *"This agreement shall renew for successive 12-month periods unless either party provides written notice of non-renewal at least 60 days before the end of the then-current term."*
- Price increase caps linked to a measurable index (RPI, CPI)
- Annual review meetings built into the renewal process

If you manage more than 10 supplier contracts, use a contract management system that tracks renewal dates automatically. Missing a 14-day window costs you a full year's contract you wanted to exit.

---

## 4. The Intellectual Property Surrender Clause

This one catches a lot of creative agencies, software consultants, and product businesses who are also buying services.

Suppliers often include:

*"All intellectual property created by the supplier in the performance of this agreement shall vest in the client."*

That sounds fine if you're the client. But if you're the *supplier* and you've just inadvertently handed over rights to code libraries, frameworks, methodologies, or tools you've developed across multiple clients — you've just surrendered significant commercial value.

Conversely, if you're buying services and the contract says IP stays with the supplier, you could be paying for work you legally don't own.

**What to put instead (as supplier):**
- *"Background IP"*: pre-existing IP remains owned by the original party
- *"Foreground IP"*: IP created specifically for this project transfers to the client upon full payment
- Licence-back rights if you want to use case studies or portfolio work

**What to put instead (as buyer):**
- *"All bespoke work product created under this agreement shall be owned by the client upon full payment."*
- Explicitly state that generic methodologies, tools, and frameworks are not included unless specifically listed

---

## 5. The Dispute Resolution Dead End

Most contracts specify a jurisdiction for disputes. That's sensible. What they *don't* specify is the path to resolution before litigation — which means when a dispute arises, you're looking at immediate legal costs that dwarf the original value of the disagreement.

Common problem clauses:
- *"Any disputes shall be resolved by the courts of England and Wales"* (immediate litigation)
- *"Parties shall attempt to resolve disputes in good faith"* (meaningless without process)
- No mention of mediation, arbitration, or adjudication as intermediate steps

**What to put instead:**
- *"Parties shall first attempt to resolve disputes through mediation under the Centre for Effective Dispute Resolution (CEDR) model terms within 30 days of written notice."*
- *"If mediation is unsuccessful, disputes shall be referred to binding arbitration under the ICC Rules of Arbitration."*
- Specify whether the arbitrator's decision is final or whether appeal is available

For contracts under £50,000 in value, this clause alone can save you £5,000-£15,000 in legal fees per dispute.

## How to Actually Fix This

Reading your contracts and spotting these issues is the first step. Actually fixing them requires either:

1. **Renegotiating with existing suppliers** — most will accept reasonable amendments if you frame it as protecting both parties
2. **Getting proper legal review** — not just a checkbox review, but a commercial review that considers your specific risk profile
3. **Using standard-form contracts** from reputable bodies (the ICC, BSI, or your industry association) as a baseline
